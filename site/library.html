The Line

When you can see the shape of it but cannot read it.

The book that names the threshold. Harlan Vance, March morning, barn apartment, dog in the yard. The name is gone — not gone forever, gone the way things had started going. The line is what you draw when the shape is still legible and you still can. The engine's narrow way is the same posture, in computational form: drawn before the moment when it would have to be.

From the opening · Chapter One The notebook started because of the dog. Not because of the water, not because of anything that came after — because one March morning Harlan Vance stood in the doorway of the barn apartment and could not remember the dog's name. He stood there looking at the animal sitting in the yard looking back at him, a good shepherd mix with one ear that didn't fully commit to upright, and the name was gone. Not gone forever. Gone the way things had started going. You could see the shape of it. You could not read it.

Book Two 2026 novel · Appalachian fiction

The Door

Even if the cave still works.

The book that names the threshold itself. Cade Wilson and Ellis, rope spread across the lap, the water below catching first light, a fray sanded with a stone. The door is the moment of crossing — what fits, what doesn't, and what gets repaired before either side. The engine's /connect page calls this five doors in. There are more than five. They all share the same shape.

From the opening · Chapter One — The Door I woke before dawn and found Ellis sitting outside with the rope from his uncle's barn spread across his lap. He was sanding a fray with a piece of stone. "Yeah," he said. "But it fixes the rope." Ellis tested the repaired section. "The rope might not fix that," I said. The water below us caught the first light. "We can't go back with this," Ellis said finally. "Even if the cave still works." We sat quietly for a while. "Rope needs fixing," Ellis said. I am Cade Wilson. I write things down.

The Keeping

What runs whether or not anyone is watching.

The book that names the engine's foundation. The engine's canonical doctrine — the keeping is the substrate — comes from this book. It is not a reference. It is the same idea told twice: once as a story of a place and the people in it, once as a computational structure that holds knowledge whether or not anyone is reading it. The Almanac, the workers, the audit chain: all of it is keeping, in the sense the book names.
