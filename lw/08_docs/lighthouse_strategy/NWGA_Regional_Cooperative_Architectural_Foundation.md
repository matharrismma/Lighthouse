# Architectural Principles

This document captures the architectural foundation of a regional industrial cooperative serving northwest Georgia and adjacent regions. The cooperative operates as a private legal entity with covenant equity discipline. The cooperative coordinates with participating churches through a relational mission framework. The cooperative produces food, manufactures construction materials, operates healthcare infrastructure, builds and holds housing, and develops the broader regional industrial capability that serves the cooperative's member base and the wider regional community.

The architecture is not a charity. The architecture is not a subsidy program. The architecture is a market structure that pays farmers, workers, and professionals fairly for what they produce and that absorbs structural risks that have prevented family farming and rural community life from operating sustainably under corporate competitive pressure for two generations.

## The strategic posture

American agriculture is structurally vulnerable to a regional cooperative alternative. Corporate agricultural operators (Bayer, Corteva, Syngenta, BASF, Cargill, ADM, Tyson, Pilgrim's, Smithfield, JBS) depend on family farmers as their distributed production network. The corporations cannot grow their own crops at scale, raise their own animals at scale, or operate the production base directly. They need farmers.

If farmers move to an alternative system, the corporations lose their production base. The corporations cannot eliminate the alternative system because the alternative is not a corporate competitor that can be acquired or undercut. The alternative is a network of farmers operating in covenant relationship with each other, with their churches, and with a cooperative framework that exists outside the corporate competitive geometry.

The architecture is structured to capture this strategic opportunity. The architecture cuts off corporate suppliers at the farmer. The architecture optimizes for Christian farming communities, which constitute the demographic core of American agriculture. The architecture grows exponentially in nodes that cannot be eliminated by corporations because each node operates with structural integrity inside a distributed network.

## The methodology

The architecture is built through a specific methodology. Begin with the end in mind. Architect both the maximum scope and the minimum first step. Design the pull execution. Complete the minimal first step and operate it well. Follow the plan until conditions warrant revision. Revise without fear when conditions warrant revision. The work is the planning Bible. Plan to execute.

The architecture operates in deliberate seasons. Planning has its season. Execution has its season. Stewardship between reception and release has its season. Each season's work happens in its appropriate time.

## The architectural commitments

Several specific commitments shape every operational decision the architecture makes.

- Covenant equity discipline. The cap table operates with founder equity diluting toward producer cooperative members across decades. Anti-dilution protections preserve member equity. Right of first offer governs equity transfers. Equity reflects sustained covenant relationship rather than financial extraction.

- Minimally extractive structural mechanisms. Every link in the cost stack is examined for unnecessary extraction. Producer pricing is fair. Member pricing is fair. Anchor account pricing is fair. Margins exist where margins serve operational sustainability. Margins do not exist as default extraction at every link.

- Generational horizon. Decisions are evaluated by what they leave to the children and grandchildren of current participants rather than by what they generate this quarter. Capital deployment matches multi-decade and multi-generation asset holding periods.

- Faith foundation as world physics. The architecture's structural integrity reflects covenant theology, biblical agrarian tradition, and the broader Christian institutional pattern. The faith foundation is load bearing across the architecture without being the architecture's institutional identity.

- Service rather than extraction by the architect. The architect does not capture disproportionate value from the architecture. The architect's economic position is among the producer cooperative members, the workers, the professionals, and the church partners. The architect's distinct role is the architectural work itself rather than disproportionate economic capture.

# Institutional Structure

The architecture operates through three integrated institutional structures. The cooperative as the primary legal and operational entity. The mission framework as the relational structure that operates alongside the cooperative. The integrated build partnership as the construction and material verticals partner. Each structure operates with specific legal form, specific governance, and specific relationships with the other structures.

## The cooperative as keystone institution

The cooperative is the legal and operational entity that holds the architecture's heavy capital infrastructure. The cooperative is structured as a private holding company over operating subsidiaries with the producer cooperative as a separate legal entity under federal Subchapter T cooperative tax provisions for the parts of the operation that qualify.

The cooperative carries the heavy capital. The processing facility, the milling and distribution hub, the preservation cluster, the rolling stock fleet, the Marion County terminal, the eventual ship and rail acquisitions, the barn depot network, the architecture provided housing, the construction material verticals, and the future mining and energy verticals as the architecture grows in those directions. All substantial capital infrastructure sits inside the cooperative because the cooperative is the institutional entity structurally suited to handle it.

Producer cooperative members own their operations. They contribute production through the package structure. They build producer cooperative member equity over time. They do not bear the capital burden of the architecture's heavy infrastructure. The cooperative bears that burden because the cooperative operates at the scale where heavy capital deployment makes sense.

Workers in the worker progression structure participate through wages, draws, and accumulating equity over time. They do not bear capital burden. The architecture provides them with housing, healthcare, food access, training, and the structured path toward ownership.

## The mission framework as relational structure

The mission framework operates alongside the cooperative as the relational structure for participating churches. Any church that confesses Jesus is the Son of the Living God can use the framework. The confessional standard is the foundational confession that distinguishes the Christian church from anything else. It cuts across denominations including Roman Catholic, Eastern Orthodox, Oriental Orthodox, every Protestant tradition, every evangelical and Pentecostal tradition, every Black church tradition, and every immigrant church tradition that holds the foundational confession.

Churches participate in covenant relationship rather than commercial contract. Pricing optimization happens through the relationship. Quality optimization happens through the relationship. The strength flows from working together rather than from negotiated commercial terms.

Several specific elements of the framework are available to participating churches:

- Cooperative supply at member pricing for church meal programs, fellowship hall operations, supper clubs, community kitchens, and other church food service.

- Cooperative supply for church members through the church as the household demand aggregation point.

- Producer cooperative member access for church members who farm.

- Cooperative facility access where appropriate, including processing services, milling operations, and preservation infrastructure at member pricing.

- Educational and training resources including CTAE pipeline integration, the apprenticeship system, open source seed work, and technical support.

- Barn depot access where barns are located near participating churches.

Churches own their property genuinely. The architecture's relationship to church owned property operates as a separate commercial relationship (supplier, coordination, partnership) that the church benefits from but does not control. Where churches acquire land that becomes barn nodes through the architecture's operational integration, the church genuinely owns the land and operates it for genuine church purposes while the architecture operates barn functions on appropriate portions through specific lease arrangements.

The architecture does not direct churches. The architecture does not require churches to participate or to limit their participation. The architecture offers the framework, and churches that meet the confession use the framework to whatever extent serves their congregations.

## The integrated build partnership

The integrated build partnership is the construction operations and material verticals partnership. The partnership controls four material verticals (timber, stone, concrete, copper) plus the broader construction services and waste-to-energy operations that support the architecture's construction at cost basis.

The partnership operates as a co-founding equity participant in the broader architecture. The partnership's leadership carries operational responsibility for construction and materials across the architecture's deployment. The partnership's existing operations (the Sand Mountain quarry producing sandstone and limestone aggregate, the existing concrete operations, the construction services) become the foundation that the architecture's construction capability extends from.

The architecture rides on the multimodal logistics that the partnership's operations require. Deadhead elimination on the partnership's freight operations supports the cooperative's distribution at cost basis advantages that the cooperative could not achieve operating logistics independently.

# Governance

The cooperative operates through a flat board structure with specialist seats. Each board member carries domain operational responsibility for a specific area. There is no conventional executive layer separating governance from operations. Board members are operationally responsible. The flat structure preserves operational discipline and prevents the bureaucratic accumulation that destroys cooperative governance over time.

## Specialist seats

Specific specialist seats reflect the architecture's operational scope:

- Production operations seat. Producer cooperative member program, producer relationships, agricultural operations across the cooperative's footprint.

- Processing and distribution seat. The processing facility, the milling and distribution hub, the preservation cluster, the rolling stock fleet, and the broader distribution operations.

- Construction and materials seat. The four material verticals, the construction operations, the architecture provided housing, the integrated build partnership coordination.

- Healthcare seat. The doctor, dentist, and vet operations at every barn, the broader healthcare infrastructure, the benefits package administration.

- Mission framework seat. The relational structure with participating churches, the church partnership coordination, the broader regional church engagement.

- Worker progression seat. The four-stage worker progression structure, the apprenticeship program, the CTAE pipeline integration, the workforce development across the architecture's verticals.

- Expansion seat. The contiguous geographic expansion, the non-contiguous cell development, the regional industrial heritage assessment, the vertical addition coordination.

- Architectural continuity seat. The founder family seat that preserves architectural memory and founding principles across generations. One vote among the board with no veto authority.

The seat structure can expand as the architecture grows. Additional verticals require additional seats. International cells require coordination seats. The structure remains flat with specialist responsibility rather than developing executive layers.

## Consensus model

The board operates through consensus rather than through majority vote. Decisions hold because the board reaches agreement. The consensus model is what Locust contributed to the architectural pattern in The Line, drawn from the Cherokee tradition of governance that has operated for over a thousand years. Consensus is slower than voting. Consensus also holds when voting fragments. The architecture operates on a horizon that requires consensus integrity rather than voting expediency.

## Architectural continuity seat

The founder family holds a permanent board seat that passes within the family across generations. The seat carries voice in governance and serves as the architectural memory that founding principles persist across leadership transitions. The seat's specific function and limits:

- Voice in governance decisions. Participation in board meetings, contribution to discussions, and one vote among the board on matters that come to vote.

- No veto authority. The seat does not control decisions. The board operates through consensus among all seat holders.

- No economic claim beyond cooperative member position. The family operates a producer cooperative member operation on the same terms as other members. The seat does not provide distributions, dividends, or other economic benefits.

- No operational authority. Day to day operations run through operational leadership. The board provides governance oversight rather than operational management.

- Architectural memory. The seat holder maintains knowledge of the architectural pattern, ensures decisions reflect founding principles, and serves as keeper of founding documents and institutional continuity.

Succession within the family operates through family deliberation about which family member is best suited for each generation's role. The seat passes through the family rather than transferring outside it. If no family member is suited in a specific generation, the seat sits vacant until family stewardship is restored. The architecture's broader governance continues regardless.

## Cap table

The cap table includes:

- Founding architect equity, diluting across decades toward zero as producer member equity accumulates.

- Integrated build partner equity as co-founder participating across the architecture's construction and materials operations.

- Producer cooperative member equity, building over time through per-head, per-acre, and patronage allocations as members participate in cooperative operations.

- Patient capital partner equity at below-market return expectations matched to the architecture's multi-decade operational horizon.

- Reciprocal anchor equity with the four-platform mechanism partners (AssetCo, Stability OS, Barn Ecosystem, Provident Precision) where structural alignment supports the reciprocal arrangement.

Anti-dilution protections preserve producer cooperative member equity through ownership progressions. Right of first offer governs equity transfers. Equity reflects sustained covenant relationship rather than financial speculation.

# Producer Cooperative Member Program

The producer cooperative member program is the architecture's primary operational mechanism for capturing the regional production base from corporate agricultural systems. The program offers an integrated package that solves the transition problem that has prevented family farms from leaving corporate agricultural dependency for two generations.

## The strategic concentration

There are approximately 1.88 million U.S. farms in 2024, with 97 percent classified as family farms. Small family farms (under $350,000 gross cash farm income) account for 86 percent of all farms but produce only 14 percent of agricultural value. Mid and large family farms (approximately 170,000 operations representing under 14 percent of farm count) produce more than half of all agricultural value. Large family farms produce 77 percent of dairy value, 71 percent of cotton, 52 percent of cash grains and soybeans, 39 percent of beef, and 37 percent of hogs.

The architecture concentrates recruitment on the mid and large family farms that produce the majority of agricultural value. Capturing these operations changes the food system meaningfully. Each captured operation represents substantial production capacity. The capital deployment per recruitment is substantial but the production value generated justifies the investment.

Small family farms are served through different mechanisms appropriate to their scale. Cooperative supply at member pricing through their own production. Producer cooperative membership at appropriate scale. Mission framework participation through their churches. The worker progression structure for family members entering agricultural careers. Small family farm participation does not require the deep capital commitment that mid and large family farm transitions require.

## The package

The package is what is necessary to get farmers to change over from corporate agricultural dependency to cooperative production and to stay profitable until the transition is complete. The package lets families make a set amount each month without stress. In return, the cooperative becomes the farmer's primary customer.

The package operates through draws against production rather than through salary employment. The farmer remains an independent producer in covenant relationship with the cooperative. The cooperative provides the market structure that makes the production economically viable. The trade is mutual. Both sides commit to each other through specific terms.

### Components of the package

The package includes specific integrated components:

- Set monthly draws against production value. The amount is calibrated so the family can operate without financial stress on a sustained basis. Typical range is $4,000 to $7,000 per month depending on operation scale and family situation. The amount continues throughout the year regardless of harvest timing or market fluctuations. The architecture absorbs cash flow timing risk and market fluctuation risk that would otherwise leave the family with unpredictable income.

- Year-end reconciliation against actual production value. If the operation produced more value than cumulative draws, the family receives the difference promptly. If the operation produced less than cumulative draws, the difference carries forward at terms that do not punish the family for the shortfall. Transition insurance covers catastrophic shortfalls during the transition period.

- Comprehensive benefits. Health insurance covering the farmer and family. Retirement contributions. Disability coverage. Dental and vision coverage through the barn depot infrastructure. The benefits package is comparable to what salaried professional employees receive. Family farms have historically lacked access to comprehensive benefits because commodity production margins did not support them. The architecture provides them as part of the structural commitment.

- Transition insurance up to $1 million per operation. Coverage absorbs catastrophic losses during the transition from corporate dependency to cooperative production. Crop failure during the first season under new practices. Equipment incompatibility with new crops. Market fluctuations during transition. Time required to learn new production patterns. The insurance is structural rather than contingent. The farmer can transition without bearing the catastrophic risk that has prevented this transition at scale.

- Primary customer commitment from the farmer. The farmer commits the majority of production to the cooperative as the primary buyer. The farmer plans operations around cooperative demand. The farmer is not playing the cooperative against commodity markets or corporate buyers. The relationship is covenant rather than transactional.

- Cooperative governance participation. Members participate in cooperative governance through specialist seats and through the broader cooperative governance structure. Members have voice in the cooperative's direction.

- Producer cooperative member equity accumulation. Each year of production contributes to accumulating member equity. The equity builds toward majority producer ownership across decades. Anti-dilution protections preserve member equity. Right of first offer governs transfers.

## Land redirection

The cooperative redirects regional farmland from commodity export crops toward food production for regional consumption. The current corporate agricultural system runs on commodity crops (soybeans, corn, cotton, wheat) for export and processing into industrial inputs. The path from farmland to food is long, indirect, and dominated by corporate processors at every stage.

The architecture redirects farmland to producing food the regional community actually eats directly. Vegetables. Fruit. Beef. Pork. Poultry. Dairy. Eggs. Grain milled for bread, grits, and cornmeal rather than processed into industrial feedstock. Beans. The crops and animals that show up on the cafeteria plate, on the dinner table, and in the church fellowship hall.

Several specific land use shifts that the architecture enables:

- Existing soybean and corn acreage shifts toward food production. Corn for cornmeal and grits and animal feed. Beans (pinto, black, navy, lima, butter) for cooperative dry pack and canning. Wheat for cooperative milling.

- Pasture and grazing land for cattle, sheep, and goat operations under regenerative management. The pasture produces meat for the cooperative processing facility and produces soil health that supports long-term land stewardship.

- Vegetable production at meaningful regional scale. Cabbage, potatoes, sweet potatoes, Brussels sprouts, collards, mustard, turnip, kale, tomatoes, peppers, squash, okra, beans (green, lima), sweet corn, onions, garlic, herbs, and specialty vegetables for ethnic cooking traditions in the regional service area.

- Fruit production through regional orchards and berry operations. Apples for cooperative apple juice and dehydration. Peaches for canning and freezing. Berries for freezing and freeze drying.

- Specialty crops fitting regional conditions and cooperative supply chain. Hops for the brewery. Barley for malting if regional adaptation supports it. Sunflowers for oil. Other specialty crops as the architecture matures.

## Member family construction support

Producer cooperative member families have land. The architecture supports member family building projects on member family land at cost basis pricing. A family can build a house for adult children, grandparents, or extended family on family land using the architecture's construction support. The cost is dramatically lower than commercial construction because the architecture's verticals and infrastructure capture the value chain that commercial construction loses to extractive intermediaries.

Components of the construction support:

- Materials at cost basis from the architecture's verticals. Timber from forestry and sawmill operations. Stone from the quarry and fabrication. Concrete from the batch plants. Copper from fabrication. The materials cost dramatically less than commercial supplier pricing.

- Construction crews from the architecture's construction operations. Labor at cost basis rather than commercial residential construction markup.

- Design capability. The architecture's standardized house designs with passive solar orientation, geothermal integration, generational construction specifications, and the integrated material palette become available without separate architect engagement.

- Permitting and regulatory support through the architecture's specialized staff.

- Financing options. Direct construction financing from the cooperative at favorable terms, guarantees that support member access to conventional financing on better terms, or specific cooperative financing structures that handle family land complexity.

A 2,000 to 2,500 square foot house at conventional residential construction cost runs $300,000 to $500,000 currently. The same house through the architecture's support costs approximately $150,000 to $250,000 at standard specification. Higher specification construction (poured concrete shells, stone veneer, slate roofs, copper rain handling and plumbing) at $500,000 to $800,000 commercial cost runs approximately $250,000 to $450,000 through the architecture. Cost reduction is roughly 40 to 60 percent at standard specification and similar percentage at higher specification.

The mechanism strengthens the producer cooperative member relationship across decades. Member families build family housing on family land at sustainable cost. The multi-generational family land pattern that conventional construction economics breaks down is restored through the architecture's support. Member family wealth that would have flowed to commercial builders, suppliers, and financiers stays with member families.

# Worker Progression Structure

The worker progression structure provides a path from work to production to ownership across four stages. Anyone who wants to work eats. The architecture provides the path. The worker provides the labor and the willingness to develop. Across the path the worker progresses from arriving as a worker to owning their own agricultural operation as a producer cooperative member.

## Stage one: Worker on architecture operations

The person joins the architecture as a worker. Operations include the kitchen, the milling and distribution hub, the processing facility, the preservation cluster, the rolling stock fleet, the barn depots, and any other architecture operation requiring labor. Wages at sustainable levels (approximately $15 to $25 per hour at entry, with progression based on skill and tenure).

Stage one is open to anyone who wants to work. The person does not need agricultural background, capital, land, or specific skills. They need willingness to work and reasonable physical capability for the operations they participate in. The architecture provides on-the-job training. The worker lives in architecture-owned housing at sustainable rates that reflect participation in the architecture rather than market-rate extraction.

The worker eats well. The architecture's food production includes the food the workers eat. Cafeteria operations at the barn depots and at the architecture's main facilities serve workers as the primary customer base. The food is the cooperative's own food at the cooperative's cost basis. Workers eat regional southern food, fresh produce from the farms, meat from the cooperative processing facility, bread from cooperative-milled flour, all at price points that working wages afford easily.

## Stage two: Apprenticeship to specific function

After some period in stage one (typically six months to two years depending on progression and operational needs), the worker can move into apprenticeship in a specific agricultural or trade function. Cattle operations. Pork operations. Vegetable production. Dairy. Orchards. Specialty crops. Construction trades. Processing operations. Whatever the architecture's operations require.

The apprenticeship pairs the apprentice with an experienced producer cooperative member or skilled tradesperson who teaches the work directly. Knowledge transfers through hands on the work in the traditional apprenticeship pattern. The apprentice continues to earn wages with rates adjusted for apprenticeship status. The apprentice continues in architecture-owned housing or moves to apprenticeship-appropriate housing depending on the placement.

### Farm apprenticeship through producer cooperative member family hosting

A specific stage two pathway operates through producer cooperative member family hosting. The architecture maintains a pool of apprentice candidates. Producer cooperative member families who host apprentice housing on their farms select from the pool. The selected apprentice moves into farm housing operated by the architecture and works on the farm under the producer cooperative member family's direction.

Triangulated relationship structure:

- The architecture as housing operator and worker employer. The architecture owns or controls the farm rental housing, employs the apprentice as a stage two worker, pays apprenticeship wages, collects rent, handles maintenance, and coordinates the apprenticeship curriculum.

- The producer cooperative member family as land host and labor beneficiary. The family hosts the rental housing, receives compensation from the architecture for hosting, has the apprentice available as labor for the farm operation, and directs the apprentice's farm work consistent with the architecture's broader worker progression standards.

- The apprentice as worker and tenant. The apprentice has stable housing through the architecture's rental operations, stable employment through the architecture, learning opportunity from the producer cooperative member family, and access to the broader architectural community through the worker progression structure.

Structural protections distinguish this from historical sharecropping exploitation:

- Housing relationship and labor relationship operate as separate contracts. Housing termination cannot be used as labor coercion. Labor termination cannot be used as housing termination.

- Wages flow through the architecture rather than through the producer cooperative member family. The apprentice is not subject to small employer cash flow problems.

- Worker progression structure operates uniformly. Apprentices on producer cooperative member farms operate inside the same structure as other stage two apprentices.

- Housing is not contingent on continued placement with the specific producer cooperative member family. The apprentice can transfer to a different placement within the architecture without losing housing.

- The apprentice has tenant rights under applicable landlord tenant law and worker rights under applicable employment law.

- Producer cooperative member family authority over the apprentice is bounded to legitimate work direction during work hours. The family does not have authority over the apprentice's broader life.

- The apprenticeship has educational substance. The architecture's apprenticeship coordinators monitor apprenticeships to ensure genuine learning rather than labor exploitation under apprenticeship label.

## Stage three: Tenant farming with structured ownership progression

After the apprenticeship establishes competence, the worker moves into tenant farming on architecture-held land. The architecture provides the land, the housing, the equipment, the seeds, the inputs, and the infrastructure access. The worker provides the labor and the management of their specific production operation.

The tenant relationship is structured as a hand up rather than the historical exploitative arrangement. Specific elements distinguish this from historical sharecropping:

- The crop share arrangement is fair. The architecture's share reflects what the architecture provides (land use, housing, equipment, inputs, infrastructure access, supply chain access, demand guarantee). The architecture's share is meaningfully smaller than historical sharecropping arrangements because the architecture provides meaningfully more and takes meaningfully less.

- The tenant has the cooperative's insurance backstop and guaranteed buying program. Risk that destroys most tenant farmers in conventional arrangements is absorbed through the structural mechanisms.

- The tenant builds producer cooperative member equity through production. Each year contributes to accumulating equity that builds toward stage four producer cooperative member status.

- Housing is part of the structured arrangement. Architecture-owned housing on the land at sustainable rates. The architecture maintains the housing because the architecture owns it. The tenant is not paying rent to a landlord whose interests are opposed to theirs.

## Stage four: Producer cooperative member with operation ownership

After sufficient time and equity accumulation in stage three, the worker becomes a producer cooperative member with ownership of their operation. Land transfers to the worker through the cooperative's structured ownership transition program. The worker owns the operation. The worker remains in covenant relationship with the cooperative through producer cooperative member terms but is no longer a tenant.

Stage four is the maturity stage. The worker has progressed from arriving as someone who wanted to participate to owning their own agricultural operation as a producer cooperative member with established equity in the broader cooperative.

Not every worker reaches stage four. Some workers stay in stage one as kitchen staff or facility operators who do not want to farm. Some workers stay in stage three as tenant farmers indefinitely because tenant arrangements serve them well. Some workers move through all four stages across decades. Each path is legitimate participation.

## Worker progression in non-agricultural verticals

The four-stage structure adapts to non-agricultural verticals. Construction trades workers progress from general labor to apprenticeship in specific trades to journeyman status to potential progression to construction operations leadership. Healthcare professionals enter at various stages depending on their existing credentials. Mining workers (where the architecture grows in that direction) progress through stages adapted to mining operations. Each vertical's specific workforce requirements adapt the worker progression structure to that vertical's actual operational needs.

# Barn Depots

The barn depot is the architecture's complete community infrastructure node. Each barn integrates food, retail, gathering, training, and healthcare functions in a single location. The barn is the practical embodiment of the architecture's commitments at the community level. Members and the broader regional community access the architecture daily through the barn.

The barn structure is a substantial regional institution. The barn becomes the practical embodiment of what the architecture is. Members eat lunch at the barn cafeteria, pick up groceries at the barn retail, attend church meetings in the barn events space, take their kids to the barn dentist, take their cattle to the barn vet, and run into producer cooperative member friends and church mission framework families at the barn. The architecture is community infrastructure that members can see and touch every day.

## Barn functions

Each barn integrates the following functions:

### Cafeteria

Meat-and-three plus deli plus diner format. Target retail price point of approximately $10 with cost stack of $4.50 to $5.50 food, $2.50 to $3.50 labor, $1.00 to $1.50 occupancy, $0.50 to $1.50 margin. Menu emphasizes regional southern food: fried chicken, meatloaf, chicken-fried steak, chicken and dressing, pulled pork, pot roast, salisbury steak, fried catfish on Friday, salmon patties, chicken and dumplings, chicken casserole, brunswick stew. Bases include grits, mashed potatoes, rice, mac and cheese (upcharge). Sides include collards, pinto beans, green beans, butter beans, black-eyed peas, fried okra. Sandwiches include pimento cheese, chicken salad, BLT, grilled cheese. Burger and fries available with upcharge ladder. Soup of the day from byproducts. Cornbread, yeast rolls, biscuits from cooperative-milled flour, lard from cooperative pork.

### Retail floor

Cooperative supply at member pricing. Cooperative produced grains (all-purpose flour, cornmeal and grits, rolled oats, long-grain rice). Protein primals from cooperative production plus Pilgrim's whole chicken from direct relationship. Coffee, spices, honey, salt. Hispanic and Asian supplemental supply through Atlanta wholesale relationships. Seasonal produce from cooperative members and from regional producers.

### Wholesale pickup

Coordination point for supper clubs, religious organization food programs, and member household bulk purchases. The wholesale function operates at scales that supplement the cafeteria and retail functions while providing efficient distribution to organized buyers.

### Events space

Community gathering space functioning as fellowship hall, meeting space, training space, and community center. Available to participating churches for community functions. Available for cooperative member meetings. Available for CTAE pipeline programs and apprenticeship system training. Available for community events that strengthen the regional fabric.

### Beverage program

Four bottled lines with cans as primary packaging. No outside sodas. (1) Cooperative soda including cola, root beer, ginger ale, lemon-lime, cream, orange, cherry, grape, and seasonal variants. (2) Beer including 3 to 5 core styles plus seasonal at brewery production scale of approximately 1,500 barrels per year. (3) Milk through regional dairy partnership. (4) Apple juice through regional orchards. Plus draft program: sweet tea, unsweetened tea, lemonade, coffee, water. CO2 from Phase 2 brewery fermentation capture. Closed loop: brewery spent grain to producer member animal feed, apple pomace to animal feed.

### Healthcare infrastructure

Each barn has a doctor, a dentist, and a vet. The integrated healthcare infrastructure restores rural healthcare access that has collapsed across most of the regional service area. Healthcare professionals live in architecture-provided housing in the community. They serve cooperative members and their families through the cooperative benefits package. They serve the broader community through appropriate fee structures.

The doctor at the barn:

- Primary care for cooperative members and their families. Routine checkups, preventive care, chronic condition management, acute illness treatment, basic injury treatment.

- Occupational medicine for the agricultural workforce. Farming injuries, equipment accidents, heat exhaustion, repetitive stress conditions, agricultural occupational health.

- Pediatric care for cooperative member families.

- Women's health and prenatal care including basic gynecological care with referral to regional partners for childbirth or complex cases.

- Mental health screening and treatment with referral to specialists when needed. Rural mental health crisis is severe and underrecognized. Farmer suicide rates are significantly higher than the general population. The doctor knows the community well enough to recognize patterns that outside providers would miss.

- Specialist referral coordination through cooperative relationships with regional hospitals and specialty practices.

The dentist at the barn:

- Routine dental care including cleanings, examinations, x-rays, preventive treatment.

- Restorative dental care including fillings, crowns, bridges, root canals, extractions, dentures.

- Pediatric dental care including sealants, fluoride treatment, orthodontic referral.

- Emergency dental care including dental abscesses, broken teeth, traumatic dental injury.

- Coordination with the doctor on patients whose dental status affects medical status (cardiovascular patients, diabetic patients, immunocompromised patients) and whose medical status affects dental treatment.

- Specialty referral for oral surgery, endodontic specialists, periodontal specialists, or orthodontic care.

The vet at the barn:

- Large animal care for cooperative livestock operations. Cattle, hogs, goats, sheep, horses. Routine herd health management, reproductive services, illness treatment, surgical procedures, vaccination programs, parasite management.

- Small animal care for cooperative member families. Dogs, cats, and other pets that families keep.

- Avian and poultry care for layer flocks at producer cooperative member operations, broiler operations under contract structure, backyard poultry, and small flock operations.

- Veterinary diagnostics on site for faster diagnosis and treatment than sending samples to distant laboratories.

- Reproductive services including pregnancy detection, artificial insemination, embryo transfer where warranted, breeding soundness examinations, reproductive disease management.

- Coordination with cooperative production planning. The vet works with producer cooperative members to plan herd health management around cooperative production schedules.

- Coordination with the cooperative processing facility on health certifications, inspection support, humane handling, and animal welfare standards.

- Wildlife and emerging disease surveillance for biosecurity protection.

## Capital and operational scale

Each barn requires capital deployment for the integrated functions. Construction at the architecture's specifications (poured concrete shells, stone veneer, slate roofs, copper rain handling and plumbing) including cafeteria operations, retail floor, wholesale pickup, events space, healthcare clinical space, and supporting infrastructure. Tom's integrated build partnership reduces the construction cost stack but absolute cost remains substantial.

Equipment costs include kitchen equipment for cafeteria operations, retail equipment for the retail floor, healthcare equipment for the doctor, dentist, and vet operations. Healthcare equipment per barn is approximately $700,000 to $1.5 million for the integrated medical, dental, and veterinary capability.

Operating costs include staffing for the integrated functions. Cafeteria staff. Retail staff. Healthcare professional compensation through the benefits structure. Support staff including nurse practitioners, dental hygienists, vet technicians, medical assistants, and administrative support. Total healthcare staffing cost per barn is approximately $800,000 to $1.2 million annually.

Across 5 barns at Phase 1 deployment, healthcare infrastructure capital is approximately $5 million to $10 million in incremental construction and equipment, with annual operating cost of $4 million to $6 million. Across 25 barns at full regional deployment, capital is approximately $25 million to $50 million with annual operating cost of $20 million to $30 million.

# Construction and Material Verticals

The architecture controls four material verticals through the integrated build partnership and through related operations. Timber, stone, concrete, and copper. The four verticals together produce the structural foundation for generational construction at cost basis dramatically below commercial supplier pricing.

The architecture is not a buyer of construction materials at market prices. The architecture is the producer of the materials at cost basis through the verticals. This is structurally significant. The construction cost stack drops dramatically. Supply security is structural. Quality control is direct. Regional integration is deep.

The architecture's construction operation is not just a construction company. The architecture's construction operation is a vertically integrated industrial operation that produces materials, fabricates components, and assembles buildings as a single coordinated enterprise. This is structurally similar to how 19th century industrial firms operated when they produced their own materials, fabricated their own components, and built their own facilities.

## Construction philosophy

The architecture builds to last generationally rather than to current building code minimums. The construction philosophy targets 300 to 500 year service life for structural shells with periodic renewal of secondary systems on appropriate cycles. The construction integrates passive solar design, geothermal heat exchange, and traditional building methods drawn from Plain community building practice.

Specific construction specifications:

- Poured concrete for everything structural. Walls, foundations, floors, roof decks. The structural system is monolithic. The walls and floors and roof integrate into a single structural unit. The structure does not burn, rot, support insect infestation, flex, or settle once cured. Service life of 300 to 500 years when properly mixed and properly placed.

- Stone veneer for exterior cladding. Sandstone and limestone from regional deposits. Properly installed stone veneer requires minimal maintenance across centuries. The stone does not paint, peel, rot, or require periodic refinishing. Mortar joints may require pointing across long timeframes (typically every 50 to 100 years) but the stone itself ages without maintenance. Architectural character that conventional residential cladding cannot match.

- Slate roofs over concrete roof decks. Service life of 100 to 200 years with minimal maintenance. Across the structural service life, slate roofing requires replacement once or twice while asphalt shingles would require 10 to 20 replacements. Total roofing cost across the service life is dramatically lower than conventional roofing.

- Copper for rain handling. Gutters, downspouts, flashing. Service life of 80 to 150 years with minimal maintenance. Copper develops protective patina across decades. Material is recyclable at end of service life with substantial residual value.

- Copper for plumbing supply lines throughout the house. Service life of 60 to 100 plus years in properly installed systems. Longer service life than alternative plumbing materials. Better water quality than systems with plastic plumbing components.

- Passive solar design integrating building orientation, window placement, thermal mass, and architectural design. Houses oriented east-west with the long axis on the south side. Large south-facing windows with appropriate overhangs. Thermal mass that absorbs solar heat during the day and releases it slowly through the evening. Reduces winter heating energy demand by 40 to 70 percent compared to conventional construction.

- Geothermal heat exchange using stable ground temperature for heating and cooling. Heat pumps move heat from the ground to the house in winter and from the house to the ground in summer. Reduces heating and cooling energy demand by 50 to 70 percent. Combined with passive solar, total heating and cooling energy demand drops 75 to 90 percent compared to conventional construction.

- Amish building methods drawn from Plain community building practice. Timber frame construction with mortise and tenon joinery for primary structural elements where appropriate. Hand-selected and properly cured lumber. Masonry foundations built to last. Hand-crafted interior details. Integration through hiring Plain community builders as contractors or consultants on specific projects, training architecture construction crews in traditional methods through apprenticeship, and using Plain community pattern books as references.
